Kindness In The Dark

Where shadows speak softly and fear teaches you to feel.

October 31, 2025

by Crystal

There’s something strangely comforting about fear — not the kind that makes you run, but the kind that makes you stay. That slow, creeping unease you feel when a story blurs the line between what’s real and what’s imagined. That’s what first pulled me into the world of thrillers and psychological mysteries.

One of my favorite literatures was Penpal by Dathan Auerbach. It wasn’t filled with monsters or ghosts, but with memories, loss, and the unsettling feeling that someone could be watching you without ever being seen. What made it terrifying wasn’t the violence; it was the familiarity — the way ordinary childhood experiences could turn sinister under a different light.

Then came Shirley Jackson’s We Have Always Lived in the Castle. Unlike Penpal, its horror wasn’t loud — it whispered. It lingered in the eerie silences between Merricat’s words, in the quiet tension of a family too strange and too secretive to ever be truly understood. Jackson’s story didn’t rely on fear of the unknown, but fear of understanding too much.

Both novels left me with a strange realization: horror doesn’t always scream. Sometimes, it hums softly in the background, wrapped in beauty and curiosity. Both authors built their worlds not around terror itself, but around the fragility of the human mind. Reading these stories made me appreciate how mystery and dread can coexist with tenderness. They reminded me that thrillers aren’t just about fear — they’re about truth. The unsettling kind that forces you to look inward.

In the end, it’s not the monsters that keep me reading. It’s the silence that follows after you close the book — when your mind starts whispering, what if?
